There is a moment I have been turning over in my head for the past week. I was reading through a minified JavaScript bundle, trying to figure out how another lab's multiplayer experiment worked under the hood. They had solved, in about four thousand lines, most of what I had spent the last few years solving in my own Svelte apps. The architecture was thoughtful. The disconnect handling was elegant. The naming discipline was tight.

And somewhere between the third grep and the fourth coffee, my sense of self started to crack.

I am headed into a postdoc later this year, and one of the things that drew me to this lab was the chance to be the person who brings multiplayer web app expertise to a group that specializes in formal computational cognitive modeling. I had a story in my head: _I will teach people these skills. My value here is the thing I can do that others cannot._ Then I found out the lab already has grad students building dyadic web apps. And I had to sit with the possibility that what I was carrying in as a gift might not be scarce anymore.

## The scarcity story breaks eventually

Here is what I think is actually going on. I built my identity on what I can do — the methods I know, the tools I can pick up, the systems I can ship. That identity got me through a PhD, where being the person who builds the hard thing is often genuinely scarce. But it does not survive contact with a strong peer environment, because in a strong peer environment, _everyone_ can do the thing. Or if they cannot yet, they will in a few months.

You cannot anchor an identity on a moving scarcity. It keeps evaporating.

The frame I keep hearing from senior researchers is that what distinguishes a researcher in the long run is _taste_ — judgment about what is worth studying, which direction to pull on, which version of the field to help build. This sounds, to methods-driven people like me, like something you either have or do not. It sounds mystical. Out of reach. If you do not have it, what are you supposed to do about it?

I want to argue the opposite. Taste is a skill. It is trainable. And builders already exercise it constantly — we just do not always know that is what we are doing.

## The taste you already have

Every time I pick an API shape, I am exercising taste. Every time I decide not to add an abstraction yet, taste. Every time I argue for rewriting a function because it _feels_ wrong even though the tests pass, that is pure taste. The entire craft of building is taste applied to small objects: names, interfaces, boundaries, tradeoffs.

What is not automatic, for builders, is aiming the same faculty at the _choice of question_. Which problem is worth building for? Which experimental question would I most want an answer to, if I had anyone's time and attention for a year? Why _this_ study and not a different one?

The faculty is the same. It just needs to be pointed further out.

## Why it feels hard

Two things, I think. Both are traps I know I fall into.

The first is that methods hide the question. A well-chosen method gives you an output that looks like progress, so you can defer the harder question of "is this worth answering?" and still feel productive. Builders get a similar dopamine from watching systems run. The discipline is noticing when the output stops meaning anything.

The second is that correctness feels safer than preference. Methods-driven people like stating things that are _true_. Taste requires stating things that are _your belief_. The first has a proof. The second has only an argument. Moving from "it is" to "I think" can feel like lowering the bar, but I am starting to suspect it is actually raising it — because you cannot hide behind the math.

## The habits I am trying

I have not figured this out. I am a few weeks into trying, which is nowhere near enough to be confident. But here are the practices that seem to be helping.

**I keep a questions file instead of a projects file.** My default impulse has always been to collect projects — things to build. I am trying to instead collect _questions_: things I genuinely want to know the answer to. One per entry. No obligation to implement. I review it monthly and look for patterns in what accumulates. Patterns in a questions file are taste made legible.

**I practice "I think" in writing.** When I read a paper, I try to articulate one thing I would have done differently, and why. One sentence. It feels presumptuous the first few times. I am trying to do it enough times that the muscle develops.

**I try to separate "cool to build" from "worth building."** When an idea excites me, I check whether the excitement is about the artifact or about the answer it would produce. Builders collapse these. I am trying to notice when I am about to build something because the building would be fun, as opposed to because the answer would be useful. Both are fine. I want to at least know which is which.

**I flip the chain occasionally.** Instead of "what can I build, and what question does that serve?", I force myself to ask "what question do I most want answered, and what is the smallest thing I could build to answer it?" It is uncomfortable. That is the point.

**I try to take small public positions.** Confidence in taste, I am realizing, comes from staking claims and surviving — not from waiting until I feel certain enough. So I have started speaking up more in lab meetings, commenting on preprints I read, writing short posts like this one. The first few reps feel naked. I am told the tenth one does not.

## What I am actually losing, and what I am not

The story I am trying to leave behind is: _my expertise is the skills others do not have_. That was a PhD-era story, and it breaks the moment I walk into a strong peer environment. Everyone has skills. The resource that is actually scarce is _direction_ — knowing which questions are worth pulling on, which fights to pick, which version of the field to help build.

I am not losing my edge. My edge is real. What I am losing is the arrangement in which my edge was also my identity. That arrangement was always borrowed time, and the postdoc is where the debt comes due.

I still love building. I still get the same satisfaction from watching a system come together that I did in my first semester of grad school. I do not think the shift from methods to taste is about becoming less of a builder. If anything, the builders I admire most are the ones who have both — whose taste tells them _what to build next_ with a clarity that methods-only builders have to borrow from advisors.

That is what I am trying to grow into. Not away from my tools. Further through them.
