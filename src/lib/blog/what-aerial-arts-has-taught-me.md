Maybe this is an admission of vanity, but when I feel defeated by academia, I rewatch my old aerial performance videos. I think about all the times I fell before finally getting it. How learning hardly ever looks or feels graceful when it is happening. And I think about how deeply my years in aerial arts shaped the way I approach research, in ways I am only now beginning to articulate.

## Finding stability in early specialization

During my college's Accepted Students Day, I saw my first partner acro performance. Something about it, maybe the challenge or the novelty, captivated me immediately. It helped that one of the first friends I made was a flying trapeze instructor, and together we infiltrated the campus aerial and acrobatics club.

We were encouraged early on to try a variety of disciplines. Aerial hoop, trapeze, silks, rope, partner acro. I had always been naturally flexible but noodle-armed, so anything that required holding myself up for extended periods was unappealing. That ruled out everything except hoop and trapeze, both of which I think of as _pretty sitting_. I gravitated toward aerial hoop and chose to specialize in it. It was the only apparatus I ever performed on, and I have never regretted not branching out.

I am someone who, once drawn to something, goes all in. Whether this is instinct or stubbornness (likely both), I tend to stay loyal to my preferences. To borrow some jargon from my field, I am internally consistent.

Which brings me to 2017, back when I was an undergraduate. I saw [someone](https://eshinjolly.com/) give a talk about their gossip study, where they had built a multiplayer web app to study real-time social interactions. The moment I saw it, I thought _this is what I want to do_. To build interactive experiments that allow us to study communication and coordination as they unfold.

That talk did not just inspire me. It gave me a direction. Since then, I have made multi-user web apps my only approach for data collection. I have not mastered it, but I know what excites me and what I aspire to get better at. That clarity has been a compass when everything else feels uncertain.

To make this story even better, four years after seeing that 2017 talk, I began my PhD in January 2021 in the same lab where the speaker had been a graduate student. What could have been a momentary spark had instead lit up a research path, a mentorship-friendship I treasure, and a methodological identity I still feel deeply connected to.

And on a more practical level, it has become something I can rely on. When I am feeling unmotivated or stuck, lost in the weeds of parameterizing a model or interpreting noisy results, I know I can return to the concrete, satisfying act of programming interactive experiments. There is comfort in the structure of a web app. The sleekness of an interface, the visible progress of something coming together. It grounds me. It reminds me what I can build when everything else feels murky.

## Weaponizing stubbornness to grow

My favorite move I taught myself was the hip hang (pictured above). It may look simple, but getting into it was something else entirely. My body did not understand the mechanics. Initial attempts ended with me sliding out of the hoop, bruising my hip again and again until a black spot bloomed where the metal pressed into bone.

I would get frustrated, try again. Get more frustrated, try harder. For a long time, it felt like I was just failing, like my body did not belong on the hoop in that way. But then, one day, something shifted. My muscles solved what my mind could not quite grasp, and I lifted into the shape. Eventually, that bruised spot went numb. Still black, but no longer painful.

Grit is not glamorous. It is boring. Repetitive. Often invisible. Progress usually feels like stagnation until suddenly it does not. Research can feel the same way. You revisit the same questions, try variations that still do not quite work, wonder if anything is moving at all. And then one day it is.

## Leveling up to overcome the flatland fallacy

Everyone comes in with a different set of stats. Strength, flexibility, coordination, musicality, technique. Some people pick things up quickly because they are strong. Others because they are fearless or unusually bendy. But no one starts off good at everything. You build capacity one attribute at a time, and as you do, new possibilities unlock. Gain flexibility, and you can enter new shapes. Gain strength, and you can hold positions that were once out of reach.

Growing as a researcher works the same way. We all come in with different skillsets, and leveling up in any one of them expands what we are capable of doing and understanding. Improving your writing helps you share your findings more clearly. Strengthening your modeling lets you ask deeper, more precise questions.

This progression reminds me of [a paper on the "flatland fallacy"](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/tops.12404) written by [Jolly](https://eshinjolly.com) and [my PhD advisor](https://lukejchang.com). They argue that many psychological theories suffer from an oversimplified, low-dimensional view of complex human phenomena. Like the Flatlanders in Edwin Abbott's novella, creatures who live in two dimensions and cannot imagine a third, we too can become trapped by the limits of our own cognitive tools. But one way out is through developing the technical skills that allow us to build more expressive, higher-dimensional models of behavior.

Leveling up quantitatively expands the theoretical space we are able to explore. Just as gaining strength or flexibility in aerial lets you access more dynamic movement vocabulary, building fluency in formal modeling or programming gives you access to richer and more precise scientific questions. The more dimensions you can perceive and work in, the more fully you can understand the system you are trying to model, whether that system is the human mind or the movement of your own body in the air.

## Adapting to different management styles

Serving on the aerial club's executive board exposed me early on to how leadership style shapes group dynamics. I was not president, but I served as the Social Chair for a few years, responsible for advertising our club's practices and performances. Each one brought a distinct approach. Some were assertive and highly structured, quick to make decisions. Others were more easygoing and collaborative, inviting input and encouraging flexibility, even if it slowed things down. I saw how those choices affected the club's morale, participation, and momentum. Too much control could stifle creativity. Too little could lead to disorganization and frustration. The best leaders found a balance, or at least knew when to adapt.

PIs, like club presidents, vary widely in how they lead. Some provide structure and top-down direction. Others offer freedom but little guidance. Learning to work under and around different styles has helped me navigate research environments with more self-awareness. I have come to understand what kind of structure helps me thrive, and where I need to create it for myself.

## Choreographing my own path

Once I had the basics down, I started choreographing my own routines. There is something deeply empowering about stringing moves together into a sequence that feels entirely yours. Unlike more formal dance disciplines, where a single choreographer teaches the group a set routine, aerial often encourages independence and experimentation. You pick your own music. You build your own story.

I used to walk to class with earbuds in, listening to the playlist of songs I wanted to perform to (posted above). I would imagine which poses would fit with which lyrics, where a drop might land most dramatically, how to thread transitions together to tell a story. It was part puzzle, part daydream, part embodiment.

But that creativity did not emerge in a vacuum. It was shaped by my constraints. What I could physically pull off without injury. My routines were built around the edges of my ability, which meant they were uniquely mine. The limitations did not hinder the work. They shaped it.

One of the paradoxically liberating and anxiety-inducing things about graduate school is how little structure there really is. There is no universal PhD syllabus, no choreography to follow. Every PhD is largely shaped by the individual. Just you, your tools, your questions, your constraints. Maybe your dataset is messy. Maybe your modeling skills are not where you want them to be. That is exactly what makes your work yours.

## Collaborating closely to form ad-hoc conventions

One of the quirks of aerial, especially compared to more formalized disciplines like ballet, is that there is no universal naming system. In ballet, a _tendu_ is always a _tendu_. But in aerial, the same move might be called a "gazelle," a "stag," or something else entirely.

This lack of standardization means that learning and teaching moves requires you to reach consensus on a naming convention, to reduce uncertainty. It forces you to describe things precisely, to ask questions, to check your assumptions. It is not unlike working in interdisciplinary research, where the same term can mean different things to different people. (What, exactly, is a [_representation_](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-020-02793-y)?) You cannot take shared vocabulary for granted. You have to build it.

That lesson became especially clear when I started performing with K, my duo hoop partner for all four years of college, and one of my longest-running collaborations to date.

We learned to mirror, counterbalance each other's weight, and build shapes together. But more than anything, we learned how to communicate. In the beginning, we had to talk through every detail. What move we were trying, who initiated it, where we would make contact, how we would time our transitions. Communication was constant and deliberate.

As we developed routines together, we also developed our own internal vocabulary. Since naming conventions in aerial are not standardized, especially in duo work, we created a kind of partner-specific shorthand. One example is a move we called "Tweedle Dee," borrowed from [a YouTube routine](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gDKwNW8JBcw) where the performers played Tweedle Dee and Tweedle Dum in an Alice in Wonderland-themed act. To anyone else, that name would be meaningless. But to us, "Tweedle Dee" instantly evoked a full sequence of coordinated movement. Who was doing what and when and for how long. It was an abstraction, a compact label for something deeply embodied.

Over time, this vocabulary allowed us to move together more fluidly, without having to explain every detail. By the time we performed our final duo act, we were in sync, able to anticipate each other's timing, weight shifts, and intentions with near-perfect precision.

That shared understanding did not just make things easier. It made things better. Our routines reflected a partnership built over years of trust, improvisation, and collaboration. Because our communication and movement were so tightly integrated, our performances had a kind of rhythm and playfulness that felt uniquely ours.

Reflecting on this now, I am reminded of [McCarthy & Hawkins et al. (2021)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2107.00077), who studied how people develop shared abstractions when working together on physical assembly tasks. They found that over time, pairs naturally create compressed referring expressions for complex structures, building up a shared vocabulary through repeated collaboration. That dynamic felt exactly like our journey. Starting with careful, explicit instructions, then gradually building up a shorthand that let us move together seamlessly. Our "Tweedle Dee" was not just a name. It was a scaffold for joint understanding and collaborative movement. It was creative expression that neither of us could have achieved alone.

## An interdisciplinary community of misfits

One of the most unexpected gifts of aerial was the community. Aerial spaces tend to attract people who never quite fit into traditional molds. A safe haven for anyone who has ever dreamed of abandoning societal expectations and running off to the circus. It is a space where things are more strange, more creative, more free. Everyone is weird in their own way, and no one is trying to hide it.

Some of us came from dance or gymnastics. Others had been circus performers, ultimate frisbee players, martial artists, or even tree climbers. Everyone brought their own skills, strengths, and styles, and there was room for all of it. That kind of eclectic, non-hierarchical energy made it one of the first spaces where I felt truly comfortable taking up space, because being different was the norm.

In academia, finding that kind of community is not always easy. The structures are more rigid. The pressure to conform, to be impressive in a specific way, is much higher. If you do not see yourself reflected in your cohort, your department, or your field, it can feel deeply isolating.

But aerial gave me a blueprint for what belonging can feel like. It showed me the power of shared risk, mutual support, and celebrating difference as a strength. That experience informs how I try to show up in academic spaces now. Seeking out collaborators who value openness and generosity. Mentoring with empathy. Trying, when I can, to carve out small pockets of weirdness, honesty, and community for others.

## Curtain call

So maybe what brings me back to those old performance videos is something deeper than vanity. A way of remembering who I am when the present makes that hard to see. The version of me who kept climbing back into the hoop, who bruised the same spot until it went numb, who built routines from the edges of what she could do. She is still here. Just doing a different kind of hanging on.
